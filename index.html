Meet the Speakers

Christa Rhiner

Dr. Rhiner received her Master degree from the University of Bern, after having performed her Master project in the lab of Adriano Aguzzi at the University Hospital in Zurich. Together with a friend, Christa also had her own company importing textiles of the Airchair (she recommends to buy some for the Champalimaud since they are really comfortable) who financed her studies. Then, she entered the International Neuroscience PhD program of the ETH/University of Zurich (UZH) and joined the lab of Dr. Michael O. Hengartner at the Institute of Molecular Life Sciences for studies on nervous system development and axon guidance in C. elegans. During this time, she also acquired a degree in science communication from the UZH and Institute for Applied Media Studies in Winterthur. Then she got a postdoctoral fellow in the group of Dr. Eduardo Moreno at the National Cancer Center (CNIO) in Madrid, where she identified and characterized key genes mediating cell competition in Drosophila and mice. In 2011, Dr. Rhiner became a lecturer and investigator at the IZB in Bern, Switzerland, where she worked on neuronal selection and began to study regenerative neurogenesis in Drosophila. In 2016, she moved to Portugal to start her own group at the Champalimaud Center in Lisbon focusing on stem cell control and regeneration.

Christian Machens

Christian did his PhD in computational neuroscience with Andreas Herz at the Humboldt-University in Berlin. In 2002, he moved to Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory where he worked as a postdoctoral fellow with Tony Zador and Carlos Brody. After a brief stint as a junior research group leader at the Ludwig-Maximilians-University in Munich during the year 2006/2007, he was appointed Assistant Professor at the Ecole normale supérieure in Paris in 2007 where he joined the Group for Neural Theory. Since 2011, Christian is a full-time faculty member at Champalimaud Research leading the Theoretical Neuroscience Lab.

Silvia Schwartz

Silvia did her Master thesis in Rome on the behaviour of mice housed in space, on the international space station (How cool is that!), then she went to the friendly New Zealand, where she did her PhD on the molecular pathways underlying long-term memory formation in flies. It was here that she learned that Sundays are not made for working, because during her second year of PhD, she was working on a Sunday afternoon and went into the Head of the Faculty lab to refill the 5 litres water tank... she obviously forgot it there and basically flooded a lab, and was risking to flood the lab below, which was the microscopy facility... Well not funny at that moment, she was traumatised and the funny thing is that people seem to be amused by it! But no harm done. Currently she is doing a Postdoc in Rhiner Lab where she is investigating the molecular pathways that underpins damage-responsive neural stem cells in flies. She will now give us some insights on her experience.

Dennis Eckmeier

Dennis lifelong dream was to become a paleontologist, but once he faced the fact that it was more about rocks than dinosaurs, he went to the red cross for a year to and then studied Biology in Marburg. He moved from a rather traumatic undergrad thesis on frog courtship together with a mild World of Warcraft addiction to work on zebra finch flight navigation in Bielefeld University. Dennis claims that his PhD was “the best time” he had in academia, but he still faced several burn out in the middle of it. He has been a postdoc for almost 7 years in Carey Lab and he learned a lot about “advisers” and how to get your great career start completely fucked up by bad decisions. So, we will now share with us those experiments, warnings and tips on what worked best.

Ekaterina Vinnik

Russian, M.D., Ph.D. in cognitive neuroscience; discovered an auditory illusion. I love doing research as much as my practical work (one-to-one consultations). That plus exploring the world with my 3 year old son and hiking is what makes my heart beat.

André Maia Chagas

Andre is a researcher at the Ophthalmic research laboratory at the University of Tuebingen, and has been actively working with open source technologies for the democratization of science for over 7 years. He has started the openeuroscience.com portal and contributed with Trend in Africa (an NGO dedicated to science education) teaching researchers how to build their own lab equipment. More recently he has co-founded Prometheus Science a startup dedicated to making open source scientific hardware the norm rather than the exception in labs worldwide. In his talk we are going to cover the current affairs in open science and why adopting them benefits research at the individual as well as at the collective level.

Maria Inês Vicente

PhD Graduate from Mainen Lab, currently working on a project to merge open science and society. With the "Plataforma de Ciência Aberta" (PCA) she and her group are trying to promote the use of open science in educational contexts, but also to make use of this ideas as motors for socio-economical growth. PCA is the first node of the internacional Open Science Centre and is a joint colaboration between the Municipality of Figueira de Castelo Rodrigo and the University of Leiden (Neatherlands).

Thiago Carvalho

I studied Biology at the University of Sao Paulo, Campinas. In 1998, I joined the Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência in Lisbon to do my PhD in immunology. In 2003 I moved to the University of Alabama, Birmingham as an Irvington Institute fellow, working on B cells with John Kearney. Four years later, I returned to Portugal to be the associate curator for the Gulbenkian Museum's Darwin exhibition. Once the "Darwin's Evolution" show closed in 2009, I returned to the IGC as director of graduate programs, which included a period as interim director of the MD PhD program. In 2015 I moved to New York to take a position as senior scientific editor at the Journal of Experimental Medicine (Rockefeller University Press), where I remained a little over two years. This summer, I came to the CCU as Graduate Studies Coordinator.

Danbee Kim

Danbee (INDP 2013) spends a lot of time thinking about permaculture, anarchy, circus, and education. She currently studies cuttlefish, develops general principles of intelligence, and builds interactive art installations that collaborate with the public to measure human behavior and psychophysics.

Gonçalo Lopes

Gonçalo is a software engineer turned neuroscientist. He completed his PhD at the CCU with Adam Kampff and Joe Paton, trying to understand what is the role of motor cortex in the control of movement in non-primate mammals. Along the way, Gonçalo developed the open-source Bonsai visual programming language as a way to rapidly prototype interactive neuroscience experiments.

Sam Viana Meyler

I am interested in the intersections between science and art, having worked for many years both as a biologist and performer. I’m currently in my final year of a practice-based thesis looking at perceptual ambiguity within physical theatre.

João Frazão

João is a roboticist and software engineer with a long experience developing real-time interactive applications. Having worked for pioneer tech startups in robotics and augmented reality, he is used to mixing sensors, actuators, and embedded systems to create next generation human computer interfaces. His projects span many fields, including technologically enhanced dance performances, interactive museums, and autonomous search and rescue robots. At Champalimaud Research, João designs high-end systems for electrophysiology. He also collaborated in the development of the Bonsai programming language.

Diogo Matias

My name is Diogo Matias, I am currently a research technician at the Neuroethology lab. I come from an evolutionary and developmental background and did my studies in Lisbon. I have above average survival skills and can build you a table out of logs, shelter, get food and start a controlled fire. My favorite form of self expression is digital art, specialty in video-game format.

Pietro Vertechi

After studying fundamental mathematics, I transitioned to neuroscience, doing an internship in neural modeling in the Machens Lab. Now I'm doing my PhD in the Systems Neuroscience lab, where I design behavioral tasks for both rodents and humans, and analyze behavioral data quantitatively. Despite being interested in game development, I'm actually really bad at playing them except 'Thomas was alone' (I'm very good at falling).

Tiago Quendera

I have only just started my career dedicated to the study of human cognition, however, video games have been a constant throughout my whole life. With that said, after I graduated in psychology and spent some time travelling, I joined the Mainen Lab with the objective to help design video games that, while able to reveal significant psychometric features of human behaviour, are as fun as the ones I grew up with. I am currently working on my master thesis exactly on this topic. My hobbies are to study philosophy of the mind, playing video games, producing music and playing the drums and, (albeit terrible at it) playing basketball.

The Venue

Quinta dos Machados, Mafra

Set in a 15 hectare woodland property, this hacienda-style, refined hotel with an annex is 4.5 km from Tapada Nacional de Mafra park and 14 km from the Castro of Zambujal archaeological site.
Bright rooms with wood furnishings and en suite bathrooms feature TVs and free Wi-Fi. Upgraded rooms add tea and coffeemakers and forest views. The 2 independent houses include kitchens, living/dining areas and balconies.
Amenities include a plush restaurant, an exercise room, and a spa area with a Turkish bath and a sauna. There's also event space and an outdoor pool.
