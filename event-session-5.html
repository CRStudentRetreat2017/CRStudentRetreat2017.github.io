The formulation of a problem is often more essential than its solution, which may merely be a matter of mathematical or experimental skill. To raise new questions, new possibilities, to regard old problems from a new angle, requires creative imagination and marks real advance in science.
- A. Einstein (1938)
What I cannot create, I do not understand.
- R. Feynman (1988)

In this session, we explore the links between science and creativity. We do not only discuss how science can influence art (and vice versa), but also look at creative ideas on how to communicate and visualize (our) science to the research community in specific, as well as the general public at large.

  • 16:00 Introduction of session & speakers
  • 16:10 Talk: Danbee Kim & Gonçalo Lopes (EveryMind)
  • 17:00 Coffee Break
  • 17:30 Interactive tables:
    • a) Animation Design for Game Development and Digital Art (Diogo):
      My table will show you the basics of some animation techniques and has the goal of helping you create an animated game sprite (character, spaceship, potato, etc.). If we have time we can also talk about more science-oriented subjects, but still with a digital art/animation background. For example, how to create a motion graphic abstract.
    • b) Game development in Science (Pietro):
      In this workshop, we'll see how videogames can help science. I'll show a prototype of a 2D space-shooting game I'm developing to study confidence in decision making under uncertainty. We'll take this game as a starting point to understand possibilities and limitations of videogames as a tool in experimental psychology. The focus will be on two complementary aspects of games in research: What are the guiding principles to design a game that is both entertaining and insightful in terms of probing the decision process of the player? How does one code a game (using the Godot game engine)?
    • c) Collaborative Data Analysis (Danbee, Gonçalo L.):
      We will provide github pages tutorials for setting up an online lab notebook, and demo several varieties of scientific documentation. We will also share several open datasets and collaboratively set-up a theoretical framing and analytical workflow for digesting those datasets.
    • d) Generative art (Dennis, Gonçalo G.):
      We are exploring simple models of biological phenomena that are able to generate surprising patterns and motives. Specifically, we will use the Processing framework as an easy coding environment to create art. We provide examples and tutorials that will guide you to create your own sketches and digital designs. Therefore, it offers you an easy entrance to the world of programming together with the freedom of individual artistic expression.
    • e) Science & Performance (Sam Meyler)
      Inspired loosely by Peter Brook’s ‘The Empty Space’, we will wear and explore a range of ambiguous masks and see how they create different inference spaces for the observer. How do we allow for character idiosyncrasies to emerge? The workshop will involve very simple theatre exercises and games in a fun and safe environment, and end (if time allows) with a discussion on biological motion and observational social learning.
    • f) Interactive surfaces (João, Mauricio)
      In social contexts different levels of the environment or subjects actions synchronize and interact. The idea of this table is to explore ways of visualizing this interactions by means of a continuous sampling of some movements made by many subjects with respect to a background rhythm that they will be using to coordinate their individual movements. Using Bonsai and TUNAs we will explore video mapping using the relations between music and movements, and we hope that this project will lead to a visualization for the retreat party.
